## Main Read

The section’s main point reads as: tariff shocks are grossly reallocative at the sector level, so aggregate effects hide sign reversals, amplifications, and third-country incidence that only become visible once one looks through the domestic network.

## Findings

1. `56_sectoral_channels.tex:3` frames the section too broadly. The actual evidence is a benchmark-calibrated decomposition, not a universal three-pattern law.
2. `56_sectoral_channels.tex:43-55` still blurs accounting and explanation. The paragraph shows where the US spillover mass lands, but the mechanism for why large service sectors absorb so much of it remains implicit.
3. `56_sectoral_channels.tex:75-79` carries too many claims at once. It moves from sectoral incidence to trade diversion to invoicing and ROW effects without clearly separating observed margins from interpretation.

## Priority Recommendations

- Recast the opening around the paper’s core narrative: aggregates are net objects, and the section’s job is to show what those nets hide.
- State the US spillover mechanism in one explicit sentence before the numerical examples.
- Split the euro-area discussion into trade margins, invoicing interpretation, and aggregate-incidence implication.

## Strongest / Weakest

- Strongest: the US/China spillover-matrix discussion, because the numbers are tightly linked to the claim and the cross-country comparison is crisp.
- Weakest: the euro-area subsection, because it mixes incidence, mechanism, and pricing-regime interpretation too tightly.
