# Fiscal-LPT literature map

Date: May 13, 2026

Scope: papers directly related to the MCMS Fiscal-LPT effort: a multisector,
multi-country New Keynesian production-network model with fiscal wedges,
tariffs/production taxes, fiscal-rule calibration, and LPT-style debt closure.

This is not a complete list of every paper in fiscal macro or production
networks. It is the directly relevant set: papers that either determine how the
model should be positioned, how its fiscal rules should be calibrated, or how
its multisector fiscal-tax mechanism should be interpreted.

## Tier 0: core papers the project must cite

1. Leeper, Eric M., Michael Plante, and Nora Traum. 2010. "Dynamics of Fiscal
   Financing in the United States." Journal of Econometrics 156(2): 304-321.
   DOI: 10.1016/j.jeconom.2009.11.001.
   Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w15160

   Direct relevance: the project's "LPT" reference. It estimates fiscal policy
   rules in a DSGE model with government spending, transfers, debt, and
   distortionary taxes. Our current calibration is LPT-style unless the full
   Bayesian DSGE estimation is implemented.

2. Cox, Lydia, Jiacheng Feng, Gernot Muller, Ernesto Pasten, Raphael Schoenle,
   and Michael Weber. 2024. "Optimal Monetary and Fiscal Policies in
   Disaggregated Economies." NBER Working Paper 32914.
   DOI: 10.3386/w32914.
   Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w32914

   Direct relevance: closest match to the paper's fiscal-policy-in-disaggregated
   economies ambition. It studies monetary and fiscal policy in a multisector
   New Keynesian model and explicitly assigns roles to sectoral fiscal policy
   and aggregate monetary policy.

3. Antonova, Anastasiia, and Gernot J. Muller. 2026. "Distorted Prices and
   Targeted Taxes in the New Keynesian Network Model." Journal of Monetary
   Economics 158(C). DOI: 10.1016/j.jmoneco.2026.103904.
   Earlier versions: CEPR DP19444, 2024; CESifo WP 12144, 2025.
   Links: https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/moneco/v158y2026ics030439322600019x.html
   and https://cepr.org/publications/dp19444

   Direct relevance: closest paper to targeted sectoral sales/production taxes
   in a New Keynesian network. This should be treated as a central comparator
   for any claim about sector-specific fiscal instruments.

4. La'O, Jennifer, and Alireza Tahbaz-Salehi. 2025. "Missing Tax Instruments:
   Attaining Production Efficiency in Disaggregated, Production Network
   Economies with Nominal Rigidities." Working paper.
   Link: https://www.kellogg.northwestern.edu/academics-research/research/detail/2025/missing-tax-instruments-attaining-production-efficiency-in-disaggregated-production/

   Direct relevance: gives the conceptual boundary for what fiscal instruments
   are missing when monetary policy alone cannot undo nominal distortions in a
   disaggregated production network.

5. Rubbo, Elisa. 2023. "Networks, Phillips Curves, and Monetary Policy."
   Econometrica 91(4): 1417-1455. DOI: 10.3982/ECTA18654.
   Link: https://www.econometricsociety.org/doi/10.3982/ECTA18654

   Direct relevance: central New Keynesian production-network paper. It shows
   how input-output linkages change Phillips curves, welfare, and optimal
   stabilization targets.

6. La'O, Jennifer, and Alireza Tahbaz-Salehi. 2022. "Optimal Monetary Policy in
   Production Networks." Econometrica 90(3): 1295-1336.
   DOI: 10.3982/ECTA18627.
   Links: https://www.nber.org/papers/w27464 and
   https://www.econometricsociety.org/publications/econometrica/2022/05/01/optimal-monetary-policy-production-networks

   Direct relevance: benchmark for optimal policy in production networks before
   fiscal instruments are introduced.

## Tier 1: fiscal-rule estimation, determinacy, and fiscal financing

7. Leeper, Eric M. 1991. "Equilibria under 'Active' and 'Passive' Monetary and
   Fiscal Policies." Journal of Monetary Economics 27(1): 129-147.
   DOI: 10.1016/0304-3932(91)90007-B.

   Direct relevance: determinacy and active/passive fiscal-monetary assignment.
   This is necessary background for judging whether debt feedback closes the
   model.

8. Schmitt-Grohe, Stephanie, and Martin Uribe. 2007. "Optimal Simple and
   Implementable Monetary and Fiscal Rules." Journal of Monetary Economics
   54(6): 1702-1725. DOI: 10.1016/j.jmoneco.2006.07.002.
   Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w10253

   Direct relevance: simple implementable monetary/fiscal rules, uniqueness,
   and fiscal feedback on liabilities.

9. Leeper, Eric M., Todd B. Walker, and Shu-Chun Susan Yang. 2013. "Fiscal
   Foresight and Information Flows." Econometrica 81(3): 1115-1145.
   DOI: 10.3982/ECTA8337.
   Link: https://www.econometricsociety.org/publications/econometrica/browse/2013/05/01/fiscal-foresight-and-information-flows

   Direct relevance: tax policy is often anticipated. This matters if the
   project interprets fiscal shocks as structural rather than reduced-form
   innovations.

10. Leeper, Eric M., Nora Traum, and Todd B. Walker. 2017. "Clearing Up the
    Fiscal Multiplier Morass." American Economic Review 107(8): 2409-2454.
    DOI: 10.1257/aer.20111196.
    Links: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.20111196 and
    https://www.nber.org/papers/w21433

    Direct relevance: Bayesian estimated monetary DSGE with rich fiscal details
    and regime-dependent fiscal multipliers.

11. Forni, Lorenzo, Libero Monteforte, and Luca Sessa. 2009. "The General
    Equilibrium Effects of Fiscal Policy: Estimates for the Euro Area." Journal
    of Public Economics 93(3-4): 559-585.
    DOI: 10.1016/j.jpubeco.2008.09.010.
    Link: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0047272708001382

    Direct relevance: Euro-area DSGE fiscal block with distortionary taxation
    and fiscal data.

12. Coenen, Gunter, Roland Straub, and Mathias Trabandt. 2013. "Gauging the
    Effects of Fiscal Stimulus Packages in the Euro Area." Journal of Economic
    Dynamics and Control 37(2): 367-386.
    DOI: 10.1016/j.jedc.2012.09.006.
    Link: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S016518891200190X

    Direct relevance: rich euro-area fiscal DSGE estimation and fiscal
    instrument multipliers.

13. Zubairy, Sarah. 2014. "On Fiscal Multipliers: Estimates from a Medium Scale
    DSGE Model." International Economic Review 55(1): 169-195.
    DOI: 10.1111/iere.12045.
    Link: https://ideas.repec.org/a/wly/iecrev/v55y2014i1p169-195.html

    Direct relevance: estimated DSGE fiscal block with government spending and
    tax multipliers.

14. Drautzburg, Thorsten, and Harald Uhlig. 2015. "Fiscal Stimulus and
    Distortionary Taxation." Review of Economic Dynamics 18(4): 894-920.
    DOI: 10.1016/j.red.2015.09.003.
    Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w17111

    Direct relevance: distortionary taxes in a medium-scale New Keynesian
    fiscal stimulus model.

15. Leeper, Eric M., Christopher A. Sims, and Tao Zha. 1996. "What Does
    Monetary Policy Do?" Brookings Papers on Economic Activity 1996(2): 1-78.
    DOI: 10.2307/2534619.
    Link: https://www.brookings.edu/bpea-articles/what-does-monetary-policy-do/

    Direct relevance: policy-rule identification and the separation of
    systematic policy responses from shocks.

## Tier 2: tax-wedge measurement and fiscal shock identification

16. Mendoza, Enrique G., Assaf Razin, and Linda L. Tesar. 1994. "Effective Tax
    Rates in Macroeconomics: Cross-Country Estimates of Tax Rates on Factor
    Incomes and Consumption." Journal of Monetary Economics 34(3): 297-323.
    DOI: 10.1016/0304-3932(94)90021-3.
    Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w4864

    Direct relevance: cross-country national-accounts construction of
    consumption, labor, and capital tax rates consistent with representative
    agent macro wedges.

17. Carey, David, and Harry Tchilinguirian. 2000. "Average Effective Tax Rates
    on Capital, Labour and Consumption." OECD Economics Department Working
    Paper 258. DOI: 10.1787/247874530426.
    Link: https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/average-effective-tax-rates-on-capital-labour-and-consumption_247874530426.html

    Direct relevance: OECD extension and refinement of average effective tax
    rate construction.

18. Trabandt, Mathias, and Harald Uhlig. 2011/2012. "How Far Are We From the
    Slippery Slope? The Laffer Curve Revisited." NBER Working Paper 15343.
    Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w15343

    Direct relevance: macro tax-rate data and labor/capital/consumption tax
    wedges used in quantitative fiscal models.

19. Romer, Christina D., and David H. Romer. 2010. "The Macroeconomic Effects
    of Tax Changes: Estimates Based on a New Measure of Fiscal Shocks."
    American Economic Review 100(3): 763-801.
    DOI: 10.1257/aer.100.3.763.
    Link: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.100.3.763

    Direct relevance: tax shock identification benchmark. Useful if the project
    distinguishes fiscal rules from tax shocks.

20. Ben Zeev, Nadav, and Evi Pappa. 2017. "Chronicle of a War Foretold: The
    Macroeconomic Effects of Anticipated Defence Spending Shocks." Economic
    Journal 127(603): 1568-1597. DOI: 10.1111/ecoj.12349.
    Link: https://academic.oup.com/ej/article/127/603/1568/5068886

    Direct relevance: anticipated fiscal shocks and news shocks.

## Tier 3: production networks, wedges, and trade/tariffs

21. Acemoglu, Daron, Vasco M. Carvalho, Asuman Ozdaglar, and Alireza
    Tahbaz-Salehi. 2012. "The Network Origins of Aggregate Fluctuations."
    Econometrica 80(5): 1977-2016. DOI: 10.3982/ECTA9623.
    Link: https://www.econometricsociety.org/publications/econometrica/2012/09/01/network-origins-aggregate-fluctuations

    Direct relevance: foundational input-output propagation mechanism.

22. Baqaee, David Rezza, and Emmanuel Farhi. 2020. "Productivity and
    Misallocation in General Equilibrium." Quarterly Journal of Economics
    135(1): 105-163. DOI: 10.1093/qje/qjz030.
    Links: https://www.nber.org/papers/w24007 and
    https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/135/1/105/5573281

    Direct relevance: aggregation with distortions, wedges, markups, and
    input-output linkages.

23. Baqaee, David Rezza, and Emmanuel Farhi. 2024. "Networks, Barriers, and
    Trade." Econometrica 92(2): 505-541. DOI: 10.3982/ECTA17513.
    Links: https://www.econometricsociety.org/doi/10.3982/ECTA17513 and
    https://www.nber.org/papers/w26108

    Direct relevance: tariffs and wedge-like distortions in international
    production networks.

24. Pasten, Ernesto, Raphael Schoenle, and Michael Weber. 2020. "The
    Propagation of Monetary Policy Shocks in a Heterogeneous Production
    Economy." Journal of Monetary Economics 116: 1-22.
    DOI: 10.1016/j.jmoneco.2019.10.001.
    Links: https://www.nber.org/papers/w25303 and
    https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304393219301849

    Direct relevance: sectoral price rigidity and input-output heterogeneity in
    a multisector New Keynesian model.

25. Aoki, Kosuke. 2001. "Optimal Monetary Policy Responses to Relative-Price
    Changes." Journal of Monetary Economics 48(1): 55-80.

    Direct relevance: relative-price stabilization problem that precedes the
    network fiscal-tax literature.

26. Farhi, Emmanuel, Gita Gopinath, and Oleg Itskhoki. 2014. "Fiscal
    Devaluations." Review of Economic Studies 81(2): 725-760.
    Link: https://gopinath.scholars.harvard.edu/publications/fiscal-devaluations

    Direct relevance: fiscal instruments, VAT/payroll taxes, tariffs, and
    subsidies as open-economy wedges.

27. Gali, Jordi, and Tommaso Monacelli. 2005. "Monetary Policy and Exchange
    Rate Volatility in a Small Open Economy." Review of Economic Studies 72(3):
    707-734. DOI: 10.1111/j.1467-937X.2005.00349.x.
    Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w8905

    Direct relevance: open-economy New Keynesian baseline.

28. Caliendo, Lorenzo, and Fernando Parro. 2015. "Estimates of the Trade and
    Welfare Effects of NAFTA." Review of Economic Studies 82(1): 1-44.
    DOI: 10.1093/restud/rdu035.
    Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w18508

    Direct relevance: quantitative trade with sectoral linkages, intermediate
    goods, and tariff changes.

29. Aguilar, Pablo, Fabio Ghironi, and Giovanni Piersanti. 2025. "Global
    Production Networks and Tariffs." Working paper.

    Direct relevance: appears in the current project bibliography as a direct
    tariff/global-production-network comparator. I did not find a stable public
    publisher landing page in the current search pass, so this should be
    verified before final citation.

30. Comin, Diego A., Robert C. Johnson, and Callum J. Jones. 2023/2024.
    "Supply Chain Constraints and Inflation." NBER Working Paper 31179.
    DOI: 10.3386/w31179.
    Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w31179

    Direct relevance: New Keynesian supply-chain constraints and inflation.
    The current local bibliography entry is incomplete because it omits Callum
    J. Jones and appears to list a final AER citation that should be verified.

## Tier 4: nominal rigidities, markups, and empirical price/wage blocks

    319-353. DOI: 10.1111/jmcb.12755.
    Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w19099

    Direct relevance: empirical markup behavior, relevant to marginal-cost and
    price-setting interpretation.

32. Barattieri, Alessandro, Susanto Basu, and Peter Gottschalk. 2014. "Some
    Evidence on the Importance of Sticky Wages." American Economic Journal:
    Macroeconomics 6(1): 70-101. DOI: 10.1257/mac.6.1.70.
    Link: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/mac.6.1.70

    Direct relevance: wage rigidity evidence. The current local bibliography
    lists 2023, 15(1): 313-353, which is not the standard citation for this
    paper and should be corrected.

## Minimal reading order

For writing the model paper, the most efficient reading order is:

1. Leeper-Plante-Traum (2010), for fiscal-rule estimation and debt closure.
2. Cox-Feng-Muller-Pasten-Schoenle-Weber (2024), for the nearest fiscal policy
   in disaggregated economies comparator.
3. Antonova-Muller (2026), for targeted tax policy in an NK network.
4. Rubbo (2023) and La'O-Tahbaz-Salehi (2022), for the NK production-network
   policy benchmark.
5. Baqaee-Farhi (2024), for tariffs and wedges in international production
   networks.
6. Mendoza-Razin-Tesar (1994), for effective tax-rate construction.
7. Leeper (1991) and Schmitt-Grohe-Uribe (2007), for determinacy and
   implementability checks.
